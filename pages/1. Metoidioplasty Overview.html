What is Metoidioplasty?

Metoidioplasty is a gender-affirming surgery that creates a penis from your existing genital tissue (clitoris and labia). The word “metoidioplasty” comes from a combination of Greek words:

Meta: towards

Oidion: male genitalia

Plastos: shaping or molding.

The primary goal is to create a penis (5-9 cm long on average) with full sensation using the existing genital tissue. Metoidioplasty is also called “meta” for short.

Meta is different from phalloplasty, another genital surgery that creates a penis. Instead of using the clitoris and labia, phalloplasty uses a graft transferred from another part of your body. Phalloplasty usually results in a larger penis than meta.

Keep scrolling to learn more about metoidioplasty and how it compares to phalloplasty.

Who is this site for?

This site was created for patients in the meta program at Women’s College Hospital (WCH) in Toronto, so some information will be location-specific. The information on this site is still relevant to anyone who wants to learn more about metoidioplasty.

What are my options?

You can choose different options for surgery depending on your personal goals. There are 4 procedures available at WCH:

Simple meta

The ligaments that hold the clitoris close to the body are cut to give it more mobility and make it more visible.

The labia minora is wrapped around the underside of the clitoris to make a penis.

Vaginectomy (Vnectomy)

The vaginal lining is removed but the muscles around the outside of the canal stay there.

The vaginal canal and the vaginal opening (front hole) are fully closed. You must have a hysterectomy at least 3 months before having a vaginectomy.

Urethral Lengthening (UL)

The urethral opening (pee hole) is moved to the tip of the new penis. Depending on your anatomy, this may allow you to pee in a standing position.

If you choose this option you will need to have a vaginectomy, which can be done at the same time as the metoidioplasty.

Scrotoplasty

The scrotum is the two bags of skin that hold the testicles. The labia majora are used to create the scrotum.

You can choose to have a scrotum with or without testicular implants.

“Full meta” is a combination of all of these options. Try mixing and matching these options on the Build-Your-Own-Surgery page.

There are also other procedures to consider, including monsplasty and testicular implants. Read more about these procedures on the Related Surgeries page.

Goals

Before you choose your surgery, it’s important to know what your goals are. Here are some questions to consider:

Am I interested in changing the external appearance of my genitals?

Am I interested in having more prominent erections?

Would I like to have the appearance of a scrotum?

Would I like to have testicular implants?

Would I like to have receptive sex (something put inside you)?

Would I like to have insertive sex (put your body inside someone else)?

Would I like to stand to pee (STP)?

Can I take additional time off work or study for follow-up appointments/revision surgeries/additional recovery time?

If I get urethral lengthening, can I accept the increased risk of complications?

Is my mental health in a stable place?

Are there any mental health supports I may need to help support me through surgery?

Are there any new physical or mental health symptoms I’m experiencing that I haven’t seen a doctor for yet?

Do I have any chronic conditions that will make this surgery challenging, and if yes, how can I prepare for that?

Setting Expectations

Meta can help put you at ease with your body and affirm your gender identity. This surgery may improve your body image, sex life, and overall mental health. Most people are happy with their results, but there are still risks to consider. You may have complications, or the results may not match up to your expectations. It’s important to think about the limits of the surgery so you can make the best choice for you.

Some limits include:

  • You may not be satisfied with the size of your penis

  • The size of the penis is usually not large enough for penetrative sex

  • You may not be able to stand to pee (STP), even if you get urethral lengthening

  • Recovery can be longer and/or more challenging than you expect

  • Your penis may not feel the same way during sex as it used to

Your pre-surgical anatomy is the strongest predictor of your post-surgical anatomy. This means that your bottom growth before surgery will determine how large your penis is after surgery. Read more details about Pre-Surgical Anatomy.

If penis size is one of your concerns, you may want to consider phalloplasty, which creates a larger penis than meta. The size of your penis after phalloplasty does not rely on the amount of bottom growth you've had with testosterone therapy. You can find more information about phalloplasty in this resource from the Gender Confirmation Center.

Risks

Like any surgery, metoidioplasty comes with benefits and risks. It’s important to understand what these are when deciding if the surgery is right for you. Read more detailed descriptions of Complications.

General risks can happen with any type of meta:

  • Wounds may not heal properly or heal slowly

  • Bruising and bleeding

  • Blood clots

  • Infection

  • Changes in sensation: numbness or pain

  • Sexual dysfunction

  • Mental health decline

  • Dissatisfaction with appearance

  • Reaction to anesthesia

Wound separation on the penis

Urinary complications can happen if you get urethral lengthening:

  • Urine stream dribbles, sprays, or is weak

  • Difficulty or pain while emptying your bladder

  • Urethral fistula: unwanted opening in the urethra that leaks urine

  • Urinary stricture: narrowed urethra that makes peeing difficult

  • Urinary tract infection (UTI)

  • Sometimes grafts from the vaginal canal are used in urethral lengthening. The graft may react to the area of the body where it’s used and become infected.

Urethral stricture (narrowing of the urethra)

Vaginal complications can happen if you get a vaginectomy:

  • Very rarely, the bowel or bladder is injured, which can cause other complications.

  • Some amount of vaginal tissue may be left behind and cause a pocket of fluid to form. This can cause pain or UTIs and may require a follow-up surgery to fix.

  • Pain in the pelvis that does not go away.

The "pocket" left over in a vaginal remnant

Recovery

Recovery time differs for everyone. Speed and ease of recovery depend on many factors, including your health before surgery and if you have complications after surgery. These are some of the most important milestones in recovery, but keep in mind that these estimates are averages.

  • Surgery
  • 48 hrs: home from hospital
  • 1-3 weeks: catheter removed
  • 8-12 weeks: back to work
  • 12 weeks: return to all activities

You can learn more about the recovery process after meta on the Recovery page.

Metoidioplasty vs Phalloplasty

Phalloplasty is a gender-affirming surgery that creates a penis using a tissue graft from the forearm or thigh (donor sites). If you get metoidioplasty with urethral lengthening, you may be eligible for phalloplasty after you recover. Please note that Women’s College Hospital does not offer phalloplasty.

Surgery at WCH

Metoidioplasty is covered by the Ontario Health Insurance Plan (OHIP) for residents of Ontario. To get meta at the WCH Transition-Related Surgery (TRS) Program, you first need approved funding through OHIP, UHIP, or an alternative federal program. Learn about the specific guidelines on the Government of Ontario page.

The process of getting approval for genital surgery is similar to top surgery, with a few extra steps. You will need to have two TRS planning visits with a care provider. One of the visits must be with a doctor or nurse practitioner (NP), and the other one can be with a doctor, NP, psychologist, or registered social worker. Specialists like endocrinologists or psychiatrists are also eligible.

Once your TRS planning visits are complete, your provider will submit a medical form to request OHIP funding approval. After approval, a referral will be sent to your chosen surgical center. If the referral is accepted, the surgical team will contact you to schedule an initial consultation.

Women’s College Hospital is an ambulatory center, which means it does not have all of the same specialists and resources that a larger hospital does. For example, there is no emergency room or intensive care unit. There are actually very few patients who stay overnight in the hospital, which limits who can have surgery at WCH. If there are concerns about your eligibility for surgery at WCH, the surgical team may request more information from your health care team. They will send this request before scheduling a consult, or between the nurse consult and surgeon consult.

As of July 2026, WCH is the only place in Ontario that offers metoidioplasty. Phalloplasty is not available at WCH at this time.

The TRS team at WCH is there to support you through your metoidioplasty journey. Your health care team will include a doctor or nurse practitioner and possibly a social worker, nurse, or psychologist. They will support you in discussing your goals, surgical and non-surgical options, risks, benefits, and health considerations. They will also help find out if you meet the criteria for surgery and coordinate any specialist care you may need. It is their responsibility to ensure that you are informed and ready for surgery.
