Recovering from meta is a unique process for each person, but there are still guidelines and milestones you can expect as you recover. Learning about and preparing for recovery will help the process go as smoothly as possible.

Recovery Timeline

This timeline shows some expected milestones in meta recovery. Read on to learn more details about each recovery stage.

Disclaimer: These estimates are averages. If you haven’t reached a certain milestone by the times listed on this page, that doesn’t mean something is wrong. Everyone heals and recovers at their own pace.

Short-Term Recovery

Right After Surgery

When you wake up, you will have an IV, a dressing over your genitals, and a catheter. Your surgical team will get you up as soon as you’re ready after surgery – usually the morning after.

Light diet and oral fluids will start on the first day after surgery once you are passing gas. During the first 48 hours, you should expect to have some bleeding on the dressing over your genitals. You will also notice swelling and bruising. The IV will be removed when you’re eating and drinking well and no longer require medications through the IV.

The First Month

Hygiene

You can start taking showers 48 hours after surgery, and it’s recommended to shower twice daily.

In the shower, let the water run indirectly over your genitals for the first four weeks instead of actively washing them. After this time, you can use a mild, unscented soap to wash the genital area. Be sure to thoroughly clean all parts of the external genitals, including the skin folds, and thoroughly rinse excess soap. You do not need to use soap inside the vagina or urethra. After the shower, carefully pat dry your genitals with a clean towel. Keeping the surgical area dry is important for healing.

Leave your genitals exposed to open air when you’re in bed for the first month after surgery.

Pain Management

Pain is very common after surgery. It should stay about the same for a few days before gradually improving. It is NOT normal if the pain worsens without any activities to cause it (that is, pain not brought on by any particular movement or position). If this happens, notify your surgeon.

Pain varies from one person to another. You want to keep your pain under control so that you can go about your daily activities without too much difficulty and sleep well. There are many options to help you manage your pain:

Ice

Ice should not be applied directly to skin; wrap the ice pack in a thin towel or cloth. Apply ice to the sore area for up to 20 minutes at a time, with 40-60 minutes in between applications.

Rest

Rest maximizes the effects of medication, reduces stress, and helps with healing.

Distraction

Practicing relaxation techniques, listening to music, talking to a friend, or watching TV shows or movies can help provide relief.

Tylenol

Take Acetaminophen (Tylenol) regularly for a maximum of two weeks. Follow your prescription and consult your surgical team or pharmacist to ensure safe use.

Prescription painkillers

Take prescription opioid painkillers if your pain is not relieved enough by Tylenol. Again, follow the prescription instructions to ensure safe use.

Genital Appearance

Bleeding, swelling, and bruising are all normal parts of recovery, but you should reach out to your doctor if you’re worried about severe problems with any of these symptoms.

Within the first 48 hours of recovery, you should expect to have some bleeding on the dressing over your genitals. All patients will also have some swelling of the penis and scrotum, but the amount will vary. Using ice on the area for the first few days after surgery can reduce swelling.

Bruising also varies by patient. It might be limited to the genitals, but it may also spread to the thighs, lower abdomen, and hips. It can be purple, gold or yellow. Some bruising is normal, but if you have both bruising and severe swelling, contact your surgical team.

Many people find that their genitals look the most swollen, bruised, or bloody about 1 week after returning home, but they start to improve gradually after this.

Positioning of the genitals can also help with swelling. Placing a washcloth or some fluffed up gauze under the scrotum (or penis if you didn’t have a scrotoplasty) can help to keep the genitals in the ideal position.

Caring for your incisions

Numbness, tingling, and itchiness around the incisions is very normal, as is yellow-red drainage or crustiness. You can apply ointment (such as Aquaphor) to the incision twice daily. A good time for this is after drying off from your shower. When there is any discharge from the incisions, place gauze over the area to both keep it clean and protect your clothes. Gauze can be changed twice daily, or more often if needed.

Diet

In the first few days after surgery, begin with a light diet to prevent nausea. Foods like broth or soups, crackers, and Jell-O are a good idea.

Over the first few weeks, gradually return to a regular and balanced diet. Choose foods that are high in fiber, avoid foods that are high in sugar and fat, and drink plenty of fluids.

When you’re preparing for surgery, it’s a good idea to stock up on non-perishable groceries, easy-to-prepare meals, and nutritious snacks.

Bowel Function and Constipation

Constipation (having less than three bowel movements a week) is not normal, but constipation after surgery is common. Taking opioid pain medication and reducing your physical activity right after surgery can both lead to constipation. It is typical to not have a bowel movement for the first week after meta, especially if you completed a bowel prep prior to surgery.

However, 1 week after surgery, the goal should be one bowel movement per day. You will be prescribed a stool softener while you are on pain meds to avoid constipation. Constipation may cause pain, nausea, and vomiting. It can also negatively affect your ability to pee, which can make the recovery process more difficult.

Constipation may cause you to strain when having a bowel movement, which you should try to avoid. To reduce constipation and ensure your bowel movements are regular:

  • Drink at least two to three liters of water each day
  • Increase your daily intake of fiber: fresh or dried prunes or prune juice, All-Bran buds type cereal, fruit and vegetables
  • Reduce consumption of foods high in fat and sugar, caffeinated beverages, and dairy
  • Avoid alcohol
  • Try to move around and walk short distances (i.e. around the block) on a regular basis
  • Take Tylenol regularly for pain to minimize your use of opioids, which cause constipation

Physical Activity

While you recover, you will need to find a balance between rest and activity. Rest and resume your daily activities as soon as possible according to your levels of pain and fatigue. Avoid movements that place tension on your incisions, like spreading your legs wide to get out of a car or bed.

Do not lift objects weighing more than fifteen pounds (or objects heavy enough to make you feel like you’re straining) until all incision sites are healed and you no longer have a catheter in place - at least 3-4 weeks.

For the first 3-4 weeks (or until incisions have healed), keep the distance you walk to a minimum. The general recommendation is about 1 hour total per day (six 10-minute walks, four 15-minute walks, etc). Stairs are also okay, but take them slowly, one step at a time.

Walking can help to relieve pain and constipation, and it can stimulate blood circulation in the legs to prevent blood clots. Blood clots continue to be a higher risk for the first 3 months after surgery, so make sure you’re up and moving around frequently.

Catheters

The urinary catheter is a tube put in the bladder during surgery. Depending on the surgery you receive, you may have a foley catheter, which is inserted in the urethra, and/or a suprapubic catheter, which is inserted a few inches below your belly button. The purpose of the catheter is to drain urine from the bladder.

If you have meta without urethral lengthening, you will have a foley catheter placed during surgery. It is usually removed at the end of surgery while you are still asleep. In some cases, it may remain in place for 1-3 weeks after your surgery before being removed.

If you have full meta with urethral lengthening, you will have a suprapubic catheter placed during surgery. It will remain in place for 3-4 weeks after surgery before being removed.

If you are having a full meta, you may also have a foley catheter in place. This catheter will be capped, and its function will be to hold the new urethra open as it heals. It will not be used to drain urine. It will be removed 1-3 weeks after surgery.

When a catheter is being used to drain urine, it is usually attached to a leg bag during the day and then a large bag for nighttime. Some patients will put a valve on the end of the catheter to keep it capped and then empty it directly into the toilet. It is not recommended to use this overnight because the bladder should be drained at least every two hours if you are using this device. The night bag can be hung from your bed or placed on a clean towel on the floor overnight.

Two types of catheter bags: leg bag vs. night bag

Some people find the catheter tickles the inside of their bladder and causes it to squeeze, even though it’s empty. This can cause a painful spasm feeling and leakage of urine around the catheter or out of the urethra. If this is happening, speak with your surgical team for a medication to help calm your bladder.

You may also experience bladder spasms if your catheter is tugged or under tension. Loosening the tension by repositioning the tubes can help. Sometimes, putting ointment where the catheter touches your skin and pushing the catheter in 1-2cm will help reposition the internal parts of the catheter to decrease the spasms.

Tips for taking care of your catheter:

  • Always wash your hands before and after handling the catheter.
  • Make sure the catheter is securely positioned and projecting forward. Check that it’s not pulled, under tension, kinked, bent, or compressed in any way.
  • Always keep the urine bag below your waist to ensure urine is flowing down into the bag.
  • You can bring the catheter bag with you in a tote bag to make it easier to move around.
  • Drink two to three liters of water per day - enough to have clear, light yellow urine.
  • Avoid alcohol and caffeinated beverages (tea, coffee, soft drinks). This will help reduce bladder spasms and dilute your urine.
  • Avoid spicy foods.

Driving

Wait at least 2-4 weeks after surgery to begin driving again. Driving requires the ability to freely perform unpredictable movements, which will be difficult immediately following surgery. It can also place pressure on your genitals and cause pain. Do not drive if you are taking oxycodone or other morphine-like medications.

You will need to make arrangements to have someone drive you to and from your initial followup appointments. For WCH patients, you will be provided with taxi coupons for your first followup visit if necessary, but you will be responsible for finding your own transportation afterwards. If you’re receiving meta elsewhere, ask your surgical team whether a program like this may be available for you to use.

Once you begin to drive again, keep your trips short. If you have to drive a long distance, make frequent stops to walk a little and to empty your bladder. Both will help to reduce pressure on your genitals.

Long-Term Recovery

Follow-up Appointments

Talk to your surgical team about their specific schedule for follow-up appointments. There may be some variation, but you will likely have regular follow-ups for a few months after surgery, followed by less frequent appointments for the first year after surgery.

For WCH patients, the surgical team will follow you closely for the first 3 months after surgery. If you have a healthcare provider knowledgeable in post-operative care, then you may have routine follow-ups with them after 3 months. If not, the WCH team can provide follow-ups until one year after surgery. After the first year, we encourage regular follow-up care with your primary care provider (PCP).

Time Off Work

If you have simple meta, it is recommended to take at least 4-6 weeks off of work. For full meta, the recommendation is at least 6-8 weeks, though some people may take up to 12 weeks off. However, these are general guidelines, and they don’t fit with everyone’s recovery experiences. Let your employer know that you may require some flexibility in returning to work, especially if you work in a physically active role.

Before returning to work, your pain should be well controlled, and you should no longer be on pain medication that may interfere with your ability to work.

If your job requires sustained physical activity, you may not be able to work at your full capacity after returning to work. Talk to your employer ahead of time about limits you may face in the first few weeks back at work. If your employer requires disability forms to be completed, please complete as much of the non-medical portion as possible (e.g. name, date of birth, surgery date); these forms can be sent to the team ahead of time, or you can bring them with you on the day of surgery.

Scar Maintenance

To stimulate blood circulation and improve the flexibility of the tissue surrounding the surgical site, it is important to begin massaging your scars once they have closed. You can start doing this at 6-8 weeks or depending on your personal healing timeline.

Massage your scars in small circles with the pads of your fingers using Aquaphor or a silicone gel. For a more pronounced scar, you can also roll it between your fingers. Remember to wash your hands before and after touching your genitals.

For a more detailed description of scar massage, you may find this resource from Hinge Health useful.

Activity

At 8 weeks, you should be able to return to all normal day-to-day activities, and there are no more restrictions on the amount of weight you can lift. However, don’t push yourself if you feel fatigue or pain, and take things more slowly if you need to.

You can return to all physical activities 12 weeks after surgery as long as you feel ready for them. If your wounds are completely healed, you can swim in a pool, hot tub, or lake.

Sexual Function

You can start trying out some sexual activity with your new genitals 6-8 weeks after surgery. Take it slowly, and do not push through any pain. You can try more in-depth sexual activities and try to reach orgasm starting 12 weeks after surgery. This includes sex with a partner or partners.

Sutures

You will not have to have your sutures removed, but they may take up to 3 months to dissolve. All sutures are expected to dissolve, but there may be a few that become loose. Some sutures may even “poke out.” It may be possible for your provider to trim the ends of loose sutures; however, if both ends of the suture are in the skin, it needs to be left alone.

Genital Appearance

Post-op swelling takes at least 12 weeks to fully resolve, sometimes longer. The area over your pubic bone may be swollen and firm for several months. When it no longer feels tender, you can massage this area for 10 minutes a day, which can help reduce the swelling.

About 3 months after surgery, you should notice an improvement in appearance, as swelling should be minimal and incisions should be closed. For some people, you may find your genitals approaching their final appearance at around 6 months post-op. However, some people may find their appearance changing for up to one year.

Sensation

During the early phases of healing, it’s common to have some areas of numbness around the penis or scrotum. This should improve over the first few months after surgery. Large patches of permanent numbness is very rare, but numbness in small areas or along the incisions is possible. Nerves can sometimes take 12-18 months to heal, and you may experience long-term changes to sensation.
