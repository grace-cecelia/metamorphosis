As with any surgery, your recovery from meta may include complications. Complications can be scary, but your surgical team is there to support you and help you through any issues.

This page includes information about complications you may face after receiving meta, as well as instructions for how you can manage complications and care for your body if you have them. This information is intended for post-op patients, and if you’d prefer a general overview of complications, you can visit the Risks section of the overview.

Some of complications can occur with any type of meta, and others are specific to certain procedures. Most complications do not come with serious risks, but some can be serious.

Serious complications on this page are found in grey boxes with warning symbols.

These complications are uncommon, but choosing to have surgery means accepting these possible risks. We do everything we can to minimize the risk before, during and after surgery.

Reading all of these complications might feel overwhelming. If you get overwhelmed, take a moment to breathe. Remember that you may experience no complications at all, and you will certainly not experience every single one of them.

Emergency Situations

If you experience any of these conditions, seek emergency care:

  • Not being able to pee
  • Catheter falls out
  • Large amounts of blood or blood clots in the catheter tubing or bag
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Increasing and worsening pain
  • Fever development (over 38°C/101°F)
  • Inability to eat or drink

For any other medical emergencies (heart attack, stroke, breathing difficulties), go to the closest emergency department.

Wound Healing Complications

As you heal, your body will experience many changes during the normal healing process. However, you may experience some challenges while healing. These wound healing complications can happen with any type of meta.

Wound Separation (Dehiscence)

In the early stages of healing, it is possible for your closed incisions to reopen. This is most common in the month following surgery. It may occur along the suture lines around your penis or, if you have scrotoplasty, along the suture lines on your scrotum. This complication can be particularly distressing, but most wounds are shallow and narrow, and they usually heal very well in 2-4 weeks.

What can I do?

The open area should be kept clean and dry to help it heal faster. After you shower, pat the area completely dry. Place gauze over the area of wound separation, and replace it when it becomes wet. You can use antibiotic ointment for a few days, but it’s not necessary (long-term antibiotic ointment use may lead to an allergic reaction or fungal infection). You may be advised to use ointment like vaseline, medi-honey, or aquaphor 1-2 times per day until it heals.

Avoid activities that put tension or pressure on the wounds. If the area of separation is a large, open, and deep wound with surrounding redness and pus drainage, especially if you have a fever over 101°F/38°C, seek urgent care and contact your surgical team.

Healing Tissue Overgrows

Normal healing tissue (granulation tissue) is pink or light red in colour and flush with the surrounding skin. If the healing tissue overgrows, this is called hypergranulation tissue. Hypergranulation tissue is fleshy and red, and it bleeds easily. This complication is most common in the weeks and months immediately following surgery and in areas where there was a wound separation.

What can I do?

Your surgical team will watch for this complication at your routine, in-person followup appointments during the early stages following surgery. Overgrowing tissue is usually treated with a topical chemical called silver nitrate (in the office) or a steroid cream (applied at home). Application of silver nitrate can be mildly uncomfortable, but most people tolerate it very well and do not need pain medications. Your surgical team will help you manage this treatment.

The management of the wound itself is similar to wound dehiscence (see above).

Tissue Necrosis

When part of your body loses blood supply and dies, this is called tissue necrosis. When you’re recovering from meta, this can happen when there is not enough blood reaching your healing tissue. This can be a serious issue, but is more often minor.

What can I do?

Minor tissue necrosis can be treated with normal wound care (such as applying Vaseline) and keeping the area clean. The area of necrosis may become hard (like a scab) and/or fall off, then your body will make new skin to fill in the gap. Your surgical team will provide more guidance about how you can care for this complication.

General Surgical Complications

Getting any surgery is a major challenge to your body, and complications may occur as your body responds to this challenge. These complications can happen with any type of meta.

Hematoma

This is a bruise or lump caused by bleeding under the skin. If you have asymmetric swelling and/or bruising at the surgical site, it might be a hematoma.

What can I do?

Small hematomas often heal on their own with rest, compression (by wearing tight underwear), and ice. Large hematomas may need to be drained, but this is rare. You will have swelling at the site, but sudden asymmetric changes are not normal, and you should inform your surgical team.

Bleeding

Bleeding is a common complication after surgery, and it is normal to have blood in your underwear or discharge for the first 1-2 months. There may be blood in your urine after removing the catheter. It is also normal to have some oozing of old, darker blood from the incision sites or fresh blood from around the urethral tissue.

What can I do?

In general, applying pressure can stop bleeding. Although rare, seek medical care if you experience a rush of blood, especially if you notice blood clots or if the bleeding isn’t stopping.

Blood Clots

Blood clots can develop due to prolonged immobilization during surgery combined with a lack of activity after surgery. Most clots happen within the first week after surgery, but your risk of a clot remains higher for about a month after surgery. If you develop any sudden pain or swelling in one leg, or you start to have difficulty breathing, you may have a blood clot.

What can I do?

The best treatment for blood clots is prevention, and there are several ways you can prevent blood clots from forming. Get out of bed and start to move as soon as you’re ready (typically the day after surgery) to avoid long periods of inactivity. Your surgical team will help make sure you’re starting to move around when you’re in the hospital. Once you leave the hospital, get up from bed and move around often at home, even if just for short periods. You can also point and relax your toes or rotate your foot in circles while in bed.

If you notice that one of your legs swells suddenly or you have difficulty breathing, go to your nearest emergency department immediately.

Infection

Infections after surgery are not common, but they can be dangerous. You will be given antibiotics at the time of surgery and sometimes afterwards to prevent infection.

What can I do?

Make sure that you shower daily and keep your surgical area clean and dry. Wash your hands with soap and water before and after caring for your surgical site. The risk for infection is higher for people who have diabetes. If you have diabetes, please be diligent about checking and correcting your blood sugars as needed.

Be aware of the signs and symptoms of infection at the surgical site, which may include increased redness, heat, swelling, pain, pus-like drainage, fever (>38°C/101°F), and feeling unwell. If you’re experiencing these symptoms, seek medical care and inform your surgical team.

Mental Health Decline

Metoidioplasty, especially with urethral lengthening, carries significant risk for complications, which can be both physically and mentally challenging. Despite positive impacts to quality of life in the long term, it is not surprising to have short-term mental health challenges.

What can I do?

If you experience significant or prolonged mental health symptoms that negatively affect your quality of life, seek support. This may take many forms, and different people find different strategies helpful. Speaking with other meta patients who have gone through similar experiences, meeting with a therapist, or connecting with a close friend may be helpful for you. Your support system, which you may have taken the time to build or strengthen during your surgical preparation, can help provide these sources of care when you’re struggling.

For WCH patients, the TRS team is available to provide short-term mental health support to you, and you are encouraged to reach out to them if you’re having significant mental health symptoms.

Changes in Sensation (Numbness or Pain)

Some people experience too much or too little sensation in their penis after meta, which can result in numbness, pain, and/or struggling to reach orgasm. Within the first 3 months, occasional shooting and burning sensations can be common as your nerves heal, and some changes to sensation will resolve with time. Although most changes are temporary, there is the possibility of chronic pain or numbness with any surgery.

What can I do?

Performing “desensitization” with frequent light touches around numb or extra-sensitive areas may help. Be patient - nerves heal slowly. While most nerves are back to working properly within 3 months, sometimes it can take up to 18 months. Make sure you’re eating well and getting enough of vitamins B1, B6 and B12 to help you heal.

If you are experiencing ongoing pain issues (chronic discomfort, pain with movement or the touch of different fabrics), contact your surgical team.

Dissatisfaction with Surgical Results

Your pre-surgical anatomy impacts your results. Penis length, body fat distribution, and labial tissue all affect how your genitals will look after meta. We can’t guarantee that you will be happy with the size and appearance of your penis or that you will be able to stand to pee. Many people will see surgical photos online and hope to have a similar result, but everyone’s bodies are different, and so are their surgical results.

What can I do?

Some types of follow-up surgeries and revisions can change the appearance of your genitals. Your surgical team can speak with you about your specific concerns and discuss any potential options that could help resolve them.

You can find out more about some follow-up surgeries on the Related Surgeries page.

Urethral Complications

Out of all options for meta, urethral lengthening has the highest risk of complications. These urinary complications are specific to people who get urethral lengthening.

Urine Stream Complications

After urethral lengthening, you may experience temporary or long-term difficulties with your urine stream. This may include spraying, dribbling, or an unpredictable stream. You may or may not be able to stand to pee after this procedure, even with urethral lengthening.

After you finish peeing, some excess pee may come out of your new urethra. Most people who have urethral lengthening will experience this dribbling. It may improve with time, or it may not. It’s important to know that this can be a normal experience for cis men after they urinate. While it may take some adjusting, it’s not a sign of anything wrong on its own.

Read more about the day-to-day of post-urethral lengthening urination in Life & Sex After Surgery.

What can I do?

To help clear urine out of your urethra, swipe up from beneath the scrotum toward the tip of the penis to gently push any remaining urine out of the urethra.

Burning or Pain with Urination

Burning or pain while peeing, known as dysuria, is common when you first start peeing after surgery. It may last a few days to a few weeks. It is normal as long as it’s not associated with a weak stream or cloudy and odorous urine.

Dysuria is NOT normal if there is a consistently weak stream or if there is cloudy/odorous urine, which can indicate other complications like stricture or urinary tract infection.

What can I do?

Dysuria typically resolves with time. Contact your surgical team if you have burning or pain while peeing alongside other symptoms or if it does not start to improve within the first month after surgery.

Incontinence and Leakage

Some people report instances of not making it to the washroom on time or troubles with controlling the start of their urine stream. This is usually reported within the first few weeks after surgery.

What can I do?

Trouble with incontinence and leakage usually improves on its own as you heal. However, some urinary frequency or urgency can continue long-term. If you’re still struggling with this three months after surgery, you may benefit from pelvic floor physical therapy, which your surgical team can help you access.

Issues with Emptying Your Bladder (Voiding)

After your catheter is removed, it is uncommon but possible to find that you can’t pee.

What can I do?

If you can’t empty your bladder and/or you develop a feeling of pressure or fullness in your bladder, you may need to have a catheter reinserted. It is recommended that you stay close to your hospital after getting your catheter removed until you have your first successful pee.

Note: if you go to an emergency room because you can’t pee after having urethral lengthening, make sure to tell them that you have a reconstructed urethra. It may be challenging and traumatic to the urethra to place a catheter through it. A suprapubic tube (which is the type you’ll have in place after urethral lengthening) is recommended for the first few weeks after surgery.

Urinary Fistula

A urinary fistula is an unwanted hole in the urethra that leaks urine. If your surgery involves urethral lengthening, fistulas are most likely to form at the area where the old and new urethra are connected, but they can also happen at any point along the new urethra.

What can I do?

A fistula is a serious complication, and your surgical team will help you manage it if you have one. They may replace your catheter or subrapubic tube to keep urine away from the unwanted opening for several weeks and allow it to heal. Some fistulas may heal on their own, but you may also need another surgery to fix it.

Urinary Stricture

A stricture is a narrowing of the urethra in areas of scarring or trauma. Signs and symptoms of urethral stricture include a weak urine stream, dribbling of urine after finishing peeing, difficulty fully emptying your bladder, more frequent urination, inability to pee, and repeated urinary tract infections.

A stricture can happen anywhere along the urethral. The images show one stricture close to the tip of the penis and another closer to the bladder.

What can I do?

If you have a urethral stricture, you may need to have tests like a urine flow test or a cystoscopy, which involves placing a thin tube with a camera into the urethra to look inside the urethra and bladder. You may also need additional surgery. If you have any symptoms of a stricture, inform your surgical team, and seek urgent care if you’re unable to pee.

Graft Failure

If you get full meta, your surgeon may use a graft from another part of your body, such as your mouth, for urethral lengthening. This graft may be “rejected” by the urethral tissue and become infected, known as graft failure.

Total graft failure is rare. It’s more common for smaller areas to fail, which typically resolve on their own without surgery. However, graft failure may cause urethral fistula or stricture, which would require their own treatment.

What can I do?

It is unlikely that you would know you have graft failure on your own. However, if you are experiencing symptoms of other complications (pain while peeing, difficulty peeing, cloudy urine, increasing pain, fever), your surgical team may perform a cystoscopy and diagnose this issue. Your surgical team will then provide guidance on how to treat it.

Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)

After your catheter is removed, you may experience some discomfort or burning when you pee or blood in your urine. This is commonly due to irritation from the catheter, and it is not an immediate cause for concern. However, if you’re also experiencing lower back pain, fever, or general feelings of sickness, you may have a UTI.

What can I do?

To prevent UTIs, make sure you’re drinking a lot of water, and keep good personal hygiene habits during the recovery period (showering daily, wiping front to back). If you suspect you have a UTI at any point during recovery, increase the amount of water you’re drinking, see a health care provider urgently, and inform your surgical team. All UTIs should be treated to culture and as a “complicated UTI.” If you still have any tubes in place (catheter or suprapubic tube), these will need to be changed once you’ve started antibiotics.

Blood in Urine

Blood in the urine is known as “hematuria.” It is normal to have hematuria when you have a catheter in place, and it will occur off and on, especially with walking. Hematuria is usually light red (like Gatorade), and it resolves with rest. Hematuria is also an expected symptom of a UTI.

When you have hematuria, blood may leak around the catheter and through the penis. If it looks like red wine, it’s likely old blood within the urethra that has liquefied, which is normal to see. You may observe this on and off until your catheter is removed and you start urinating regularly through your penis. It is normal to have intermittent hematuria for about 3 months postop.

What can I do?

Hematuria is not normal if it’s high volume (for example, a soaked washcloth with bright red blood) and ketchup-like. If you experience this, contact your surgeon, and you may need to seek emergency room management.

After your catheter is removed and your penis heals, you will likely stop seeing blood in your urine in 3-6 months without having to take any further steps. If hematuria persists beyond 3-6 months, you may need to have a cystoscopy.

Bladder Spasm

Bladder spasms are involuntary tightening of the bladder muscle. This is often felt as stomach cramps or having the urge to pee. Urine may leak around the catheter when a bladder spasm occurs.

What can I do?

Increase your fluid intake. Try repositioning the catheter slightly (put ointment at the spot where the catheter meets the skin or urethra and then push the catheter 1-2cm deeper into your body). If the spasms are persistent and bothersome, reach out to your surgical team for a medication to help calm the bladder (e.g. oxybutynin).

While uncomfortable, bladder spasms are often managed with medication, proper catheter care, and increased fluid intake.

To read more about catheters, visit the Recovery page.

Vaginal Complications

These complications can happen if you get a vaginectomy.

Vaginal Remnant

If you have a vaginectomy, all vaginal tissue should be removed. However, a vaginal remnant is when vaginal tissue remains in your body even after a vaginectomy. This can occur for two reasons. Either not all of the vagina was removed at the time of surgery, or you have a stricture that causes increased pressure in the area where the vagina was closed, which reopens it.

What can I do?

Inform your surgical team if you are having increased pain or pressure feeling behind your penis or scrotum, UTIs that won’t go away, or prolonged or delayed dribbling of urine after peeing. Your surgical team will then assess you for this complication. It may require a follow-up surgery to fix.

Bladder or Rectal Injury

These are very rare but very serious complications. There is a chance that your bladder or rectum may be injured during a vaginectomy. If your surgical team identifies this, they will repair it during your surgery. If your surgical team does not identify the injury, it can cause other complications, such as a fistula or peritonitis (inflammation in the lining of your abdomen), during recovery.

What can I do?

Your surgical team will identify injuries during your follow-up care, and it will be fixed in another surgery if needed. For WCH patients, this follow-up procedure may need to take place at another hospital nearby that has more resources and support.

Implant Complications

This complication can happen if you get a scrotoplasty with testicular implants, which are placed in a second surgery at least six months later than your first procedure. Read more in Related Surgeries.

Implant Extrusion/Expulsion

After testicular implants are placed in your scrotum, they’re not securely held in place for a few weeks until your body heals around them. If your scrotum heals while an implant is in the wrong place (higher than it should be), the implant can regularly become dislodged and move out of the scrotum. You will likely be able to tell if an implant is in the wrong place if you feel your scrotum.

What can I do?

Contact your surgical team. You may need a follow-up surgery to return the implant to the proper place in your scrotum and prevent it from moving again.

If you’re feeling overwhelmed by all of the potential complications, remember that this list represents every possible complication for meta, and no one person experiences every single complication listed here. Complications are scary, but there is support available for you.

Whether you’re still preparing for surgery or you’re in recovery, don’t hesitate to talk to your surgical team if you’re worried about complications.
