Depending on your goals, you may want or need to receive other surgeries before, after, or alongside meta. Some of those surgeries are explained here.

Hysterectomy

A hysterectomy is the surgical removal of the uterus. It usually also includes the removal of the fallopian tubes (known as “salpingectomy”), and you can also talk to your surgeon about having one or both ovaries removed.

Even if it’s not required, many people choose to have a hysterectomy anyway. If you have a hysterectomy, it must be completed at least three months (ideally 6 months) before you have meta, whether it’s required for your surgery or not. You can also have a hysterectomy after you get meta (without vaginectomy), but you will need to wait at least 6 months after your surgery.

When you have a hysterectomy, most gynecologists leave the vagina as long as possible. However, you can talk to your gynecologist about making the vagina shorter if you’re not using it, especially if you plan to get a vaginectomy. Though it’s not standard practice for many surgeons, this option is available for you.

Note: you may be interested in an oophorectomy and/or salpingectomy as well. An oophorectomy is a surgery that removes your ovaries. A salpingectomy is a surgery that removes your uterine tubes (fallopian tubes). A salpingectomy may reduce your risk of ovarian cancer significantly. Both of these surgeries can be done at the same time as a hysterectomy. Talk to your gynecologist about these options to understand all of the risks and benefits for you.

Monsplasty

The mons is the natural fat pad over the pubic bone. If it’s large, your surgeon may discuss removing some of this skin and fat so that the penis will be more visible, but you are not required to have a monsplasty even if you have a larger fat pad. If you want to have a monsplasty, it will be performed at least 6 months after the initial meta. You will have a thin scar where the fat was removed.

Testicular Implants

Testicular implants are sometimes placed into the scrotum to give it a more full or natural appearance. Depending on your surgeon, you may have the option of saline or silicone implants - WCH patients can choose either one. The biggest difference between the types of implants is the way they feel: saline implants feel more firm, while silicone implants feel more soft.

Bifid Scrotoplasty

At WCH, the default technique for scrotoplasty is a rotational scrotoplasty, also known as the Ghent technique. It creates a pouch-like scrotum that sits below the penis by moving parts of the labia majora. In a bifid scrotoplasty, the labia are joined at the middle without being moved. If you get this type of scrotoplasty, you can have implants placed immediately instead of having to wait 6 months.

Some patients choose to have the implants placed directly into the labia majora without joining them in the center. This gives an appearance similar to a bifid scrotoplasty. This option is available for patients who are not having urethral lengthening.

Some people find that the placement of the scrotum in bifid scrotoplasty can get in the way of sitting or doing certain activities, like riding a bike. However, it is still an option available to anyone who wants it.

Erectile Tissue Release

Some people will use the term “erectile tissue release” to mean the same thing as “simple meta.” At WCH, “simple meta” refers to cutting the ligaments below the clitoris to lengthen it into a penis, then using the labia minora to add some girth to the penis. Erectile tissue release consists of cutting the ligaments above the clitoris to lengthen it.

This procedure is not performed at WCH. If you are not a WCH patient, talk to your surgical team about how they perform these procedures and what you can expect.

Phalloplasty

Phalloplasty is a gender-affirming surgery that creates a penis using a graft from the forearm or thigh. If you get metoidioplasty with urethral lengthening, you may be eligible for phalloplasty after you recover.

Please note that WCH does not offer phalloplasty at this time.

If you want to learn more about phalloplasty, you can read about it on this resource from Trans Care BC.
